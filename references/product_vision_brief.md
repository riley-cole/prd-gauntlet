# Product Vision Brief — Phase 5 Reference

Extraction methodology for Phase 5 (Product Vision Brief). This phase is a guided conversation that pulls the product vision out of the PM's head and onto paper through structured questioning, then synthesizes the answers into a polished standalone document.

The PM should never stare at a blank template. They should have a conversation that produces the document.

## Why This Phase Exists

The gap between "concept confirmed" (Phase 4) and "component map" (Phase 7) is where non-engineering stakeholders get lost. A designer picking up a Phase 8 PRD reads a build spec when what they need is the product intent, the emotional target, the user archetypes, and the key moments that matter.

The Product Vision Brief is the document that anyone outside the PRD process can pick up and fundamentally move the ball forward in their domain. They might land 60% of it or 140% of it. But the shape and sense of it is locked enough that they can start their processes and deliver value.

This is a focused effort to extract the product vision and concept out of a PM's brain and onto paper.

## What It Is

- The product thesis in plain language (not engineering language)
- User archetypes with enough depth that a designer can empathize, not just categorize
- Key moments described at the experience level, not the implementation level
- External references decomposed into specific, transferable principles
- Design intent specific enough to start visual exploration
- Scope framed as philosophy, not feature lists

## What It Is Not

- A data model
- A component map
- User stories with acceptance criteria
- A build sequence
- A design system (hex codes, font stacks, spacing scales)
- A list of "similar apps" the PM thinks this could be

## Who It Unlocks

- A **designer** can start visual exploration, mood boards, screen concepts, design consultation
- A **marketer** can start positioning and messaging
- A **collaborator or co-founder** can evaluate whether they want to build this
- A **friend** can understand the product well enough to give useful feedback (not "sounds cool")
- **Phases 6-8** get sharper because design thinking has already started

## The Extraction Process

This is not "fill in the template." It is seven extraction rounds, one per document section. Each round follows the same discipline: ask, push, synthesize, confirm, lock.

### Extraction Discipline

Borrowed from the Gauntlet's core methodology and sharpened for vision work:

**Lead with a position, not a question.** Don't ask "what's your product thesis?" Say "Based on everything we've discussed, I think your product thesis is [X]. Here's where I think it's sharp and where it's still fuzzy." The owner reacts to a draft, not a blank page. Reacting is easier than creating, and it surfaces disagreements faster.

**One round at a time.** Never batch multiple sections. Complete one extraction round (ask, push, synthesize, confirm) before starting the next. Batched rounds produce shallow answers across the board instead of deep answers where they matter.

**Push once on vagueness, then accept and note.** The first answer is usually the polished version. Push once with a specific follow-up. If the second answer is still vague, accept it and note the gap in Open Questions rather than exhausting the owner. Two pushes maximum per round.

**Lock before moving on.** Each round ends with the owner confirming the synthesized section. "Does this capture it?" is explicit. No ambiguity carries forward.

**Smart-skip.** If Phases 1-4 already produced a clear answer for a section, say so: "Based on Phase 3 decision #7, I already have [X] for this section. Let me draft it and you confirm." Don't re-interrogate what's already locked.

**Quote the owner's words.** When the owner says something well during extraction, capture the exact phrasing. "I take photos on my phone but then have to go find them" is better than any product summary the agent could write. The brief should sound like the owner at their most articulate, not like the agent.

**Escape hatch.** If the owner wants to accelerate: "These rounds matter because the brief is the handoff document. A vague brief produces a vague design system. Let me do two more rounds, then I'll draft the rest from what I have." If they push again, draft the remaining sections from available context and flag gaps. Never skip the Reference Decomposition round (Round 4). That round is to the vision brief what Phase 3 is to the Gauntlet: the one you cannot skip.

### Anti-Sycophancy Rules

During extraction, never say:
- "That's a great vision" — take a position on whether it's clear enough to hand off
- "There are many ways to think about this" — pick one and state what would change your mind
- "That could work" — say whether it WILL communicate to a designer, and what's missing if it won't

Always:
- Take a position on every answer. State what you believe and what evidence would change it.
- Challenge the strongest version of the owner's claim, not a strawman.
- Name when an answer is sharp enough to ship vs when it needs another push.

### Calibrated acknowledgment

When the owner gives a specific, visceral answer, name what was useful and move to the next round. "That's the sentence that goes in the brief verbatim. Moving on." Don't linger. The best reward for a strong answer is a harder follow-up.

## The Seven Extraction Rounds

### Round 1: The Product Thesis

**What you're extracting:** What this product IS, in plain language. Who it's for, why now, and what changes in their life.

**Opening move:** Draft a thesis from Phases 1-4 and present it. "Based on everything so far, here's my one-paragraph version of what you're building: [draft]. Where is this right? Where does it miss?"

**Push patterns:**
- If the answer is feature-focused ("it tracks golf rounds with weather data"): "That's what it DOES. What does the user GET? What changes in their life?"
- If the answer is abstract ("it makes golf more meaningful"): "Can you tell me that in a way where I can picture someone using it? When do they open it? What do they see? What do they feel?"
- If the answer uses jargon or internal language: "Would your mom understand that sentence? Rewrite it for someone smart who doesn't know this domain."

**The dinner test:** The final thesis should pass this: if the owner explained this product over dinner to a friend who doesn't golf, would the friend understand what it is and why someone would want it? If the friend would say "so it's like a scorecard app?" then the thesis isn't clear enough.

**Synthesize:** Write the Product Thesis section. Read it back. Owner confirms or revises.

**Lock:** "Does this capture what you're building? If a designer reads only this paragraph, will they understand the product?"

---

### Round 2: The User and Their World

**What you're extracting:** Real people, not personas. Their lives, their frustrations, their workarounds. Enough depth that a designer reading this feels like they KNOW these users.

**Opening move:** Name the users already identified in Phases 1-4. "So far we have [names/archetypes]. I'm going to ask you about each one. Start with the one you know best."

**Push patterns:**
- If the answer is a category ("avid golfers who play weekly"): "Name one. What's their name? What do they do for work? When do they play? What app do they use now?"
- If the answer is demographic ("men 30-55 with disposable income"): "Demographics don't use products. People do. Tell me about ONE person who'd download this on day one."
- If the answer stops at behavior ("they track their handicap"): "What's their morning like on a golf day? What happens after the round? Where do the memories go?"
- If only one user type is described: "Who's the OTHER user? The one who uses this differently? What does the low-effort version of this user look like?"

**The empathy test:** Could a designer who reads this section sketch a day-in-the-life storyboard for each user? If not, it's too abstract.

**Synthesize:** Write the User and Their World section. Quote the owner's words where they captured something well.

**Lock:** "If a designer reads these user descriptions, will they feel like they know these people? Or will they still need to ask you questions?"

---

### Round 3: The Key Moments

**What you're extracting:** The 5-8 moments that define the product experience. Not screens. Not features. Moments where the user feels something.

**Opening move:** "Close your eyes for a second. Picture someone using this product for the first time. What's the moment where they go 'oh wow, this is different'? Describe exactly what they see and what they feel."

**Push patterns:**
- If the answer is a feature ("they see their weather data auto-populated"): "That's the mechanism. What's the FEELING? Is it surprise? Relief? Delight? Nostalgia? Name the emotion."
- If only happy-path moments are described: "What about the moment they come back 6 months later and browse old rounds? What about showing it to a friend? What about the first time they open it and have zero rounds logged?"
- If moments are generic ("a clean, intuitive experience"): "Every app claims that. What's the moment that's YOURS? The one no other golf app has? The one that makes someone text their friend 'you have to see this app'?"
- If the answer stops at one moment: "That's moment one. What are the other four? Walk me through the full lifecycle: before the round, after the round, weeks later, a year later."

**The mundane test:** For each moment, ask: "What's the version of this that every other app ships? What's the boring default?" Then: "What's YOUR version? What makes it magic instead of mundane?" The gap between mundane and magic is the design brief for that moment.

**Magic vs mundane format:** Each moment gets documented with both versions. This gives a designer the negative space (what NOT to do) alongside the positive target. The mundane version is often more useful than the magic version because it names the specific trap to avoid.

**Synthesize:** Write the Key Moments section with the magic/mundane structure.

**Lock:** "These are the moments a designer will optimize for. If they nail these 5-8 moments, does the product succeed? Are any missing?"

---

### Round 4: The Reference Decomposition

**What you're extracting:** The specific, transferable principles from every external reference the owner has cited. This is the round you cannot skip.

**Why this round matters most:** People say "I want something like Uber and Airbnb with a sprinkle of Duolingo." Nobody knows what that means until you pull it apart. A designer hearing "like Flighty" will interpret that through their own experience of Flighty, which may be completely different from what the owner means. The decomposition eliminates that gap.

**Opening move:** List every external reference mentioned in Phases 1-4. "You've referenced [Flighty, Slopes, Oura, Apple Fitness summaries, etc.]. I'm going to go through each one. For each, I need to understand exactly what you're drawn to and what you're not."

**For each reference, three questions (one at a time):**

1. **"When you say 'like [Reference]', what specifically do you mean? Not the whole app. The specific thing you want to steal."**
   - Push if vague: "You said 'the Flighty experience.' That's 50 different design decisions. Which THREE decisions are you actually drawn to?"
   - Push if feature-level: "That's what Flighty DOES. I need to know what about how they do it you're drawn to. Is it the visual style? The information density? The way data reveals as you scroll? The restraint?"

2. **"What about [Reference] do you explicitly NOT want?"**
   - This question often produces more useful answers than question 1. Knowing what to avoid is design direction.
   - Push if blank: "There must be something. No app is 100% right for your product. What would feel wrong if you copied it directly?"

3. **"How does this principle show up in YOUR product? Not 'similarly.' Concretely. On which screen, in which moment, doing what?"**
   - Push if abstract: "You said 'progressive disclosure like Flighty.' Where in your app does someone swipe up to reveal more? What's hidden at first? What reveals? This has to be specific enough that an engineer could build it."

**The translation test:** After decomposing a reference, read the translation back. "So from Flighty, you're taking [X, Y, Z], not taking [A, B], and it shows up as [specific application]. A designer reading this would know exactly what to do. Right?"

**Synthesize:** Write the Reference Decomposition section. Each reference gets: taking, not taking, translation.

**Lock:** "If a designer reads these decompositions and has never used [Reference], will they understand what you mean? Or do they need to download the app first?" If the latter, the decomposition isn't specific enough.

---

### Round 5: Design Intent

**What you're extracting:** The emotional and visual direction. Not a design system. The mood, the feeling, the anti-patterns. Enough for a designer to start exploring in the right direction.

**Opening move:** "Forget the product for a second. Describe the FEELING you want someone to have when they open this app. Not what they see. What they feel. Use sensory words. Is it warm? Cool? Calm? Energizing? Luxurious? Playful?"

**Push patterns:**
- If the answer is visual ("dark mode with green accents"): "That's a color choice, not a mood. WHY dark? What feeling does dark create that light doesn't? Is it 'premium nightclub' dark or 'cozy study' dark or 'NASA mission control' dark?"
- If the answer is generic ("clean and modern"): "Every app says clean and modern. Show me an app that has the FEEL you want. Not the features. The feel. The way it makes you feel when you open it."
- If the answer names an app without decomposing: "You said 'like Oura's feel.' What specifically? The dark background with the glowing rings? The single-metric focus? The way it greets you in the morning? Pin it down."

**The anti-pattern extraction:** "What would make this feel WRONG? What are the visual decisions that would kill the vibe? Name specific things: a color, a layout pattern, a UI element, an animation style that would make you cringe." Anti-patterns are often more actionable than positive direction because they draw hard boundaries.

**Synthesize:** Write the Design Intent section: mood (sensory), visual references (named), anti-patterns (specific), key principles (derived from the reference decomposition in Round 4).

**Lock:** "If a designer starts a mood board from this section, will they come back with something in the right universe? Or is there a 50/50 chance they go in the wrong direction?"

---

### Round 6: Scope and Anti-Scope

**What you're extracting:** The product philosophy that lets anyone make independent scope decisions that align. Not a feature list. A decision-making framework.

**Opening move:** Draft scope philosophy from Phase 4 stress test. "Based on the stress test, here's my one-sentence scope statement: [draft]. And here's the anti-scope: [draft]. Are these right?"

**Push patterns:**
- If scope is a feature list ("V1 has round tracking, weather, photos, insights"): "That's what V1 includes. I need the PRINCIPLE. When someone on your team asks 'should we add X?', what's the one-sentence test they apply?"
- If anti-scope is vague ("we won't build social features yet"): "Why not? Not 'because it's V2.' Why is social wrong for THIS product right now? What would it break?"
- If scope and anti-scope don't create a clear boundary: "Imagine a smart engineer who's been working on this for 2 weeks. They have a great idea for a feature. How do they know, without asking you, whether it's in scope or out?"

**The independent decision test:** The scope statement should enable a designer, marketer, or engineer to make 80% of scope calls on their own. If every scope question requires the PM's input, the statement isn't specific enough.

**Synthesize:** Write the Scope and Anti-Scope section as philosophy, not lists.

**Lock:** "If a designer adds a feature to their mockup that you didn't ask for, would this scope statement tell them whether it belongs?"

---

### Round 7: Open Questions and Risks

**What you're extracting:** Honest acknowledgment of what isn't locked. Named, not hidden.

**Opening move:** "Here's everything I think is still unresolved: [list from Phases 1-4 plus gaps noted during Rounds 1-6]. What am I missing? What keeps you up at night about this product?"

**Push patterns:**
- If the answer is "nothing, I think we've covered everything": "Really? You have no uncertainty about [monetization / user acquisition / technical feasibility / design direction / competitive response]? Name one thing where you're making a bet rather than executing a known playbook."
- If risks are minimized: "You said [X] is a risk but 'we'll figure it out.' What specifically would figuring it out look like? What's the worst case if you DON'T figure it out?"

**Synthesize:** Write the Open Questions section. For each: the question, why it matters (what depends on it), current best guess.

**Lock:** "Is this an honest list? If a collaborator reads this, will they trust that you're not hiding uncertainty?"

---

## After Extraction: Assembly and Polish

Once all seven rounds are complete, assemble the full brief:

1. **Combine** all seven synthesized sections into the template structure.
2. **Polish for voice.** The brief is a standalone document. It should read as a coherent narrative, not seven disconnected sections. Add transitions. Ensure the owner's voice (their quoted words) carries through.
3. **Run the handoff test.** Read the complete brief and ask: "If I emailed this document — and ONLY this document — to a designer who knows nothing about this project, could they start working?" If no, identify what's missing.
4. **Load `.context/voice_output.md`** if the brief will be shared externally. Apply voice guidelines.
5. **Present to owner** for final review and lock.

## Writing Standards

- **Plain language.** No jargon, no acronyms without definition, no internal shorthand.
- **Concrete over abstract.** "Chambers Bay Aug 10, 2025 Score: 81" is concrete. "Rounds are presented in a list view" is abstract.
- **User's voice.** Quote the owner's words where they captured something well. The brief should sound like the owner at their most articulate.
- **Visual references welcome.** Name specific apps, screenshots, designs, brands. Show the reader what the target feels like, not just what it does.

## Template

```markdown
# [Product Name] — Product Vision Brief

Version: [X]
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Author: [owner name]
Status: [DRAFT / LOCKED]

---

## 1. The Product Thesis

What this is, who it's for, why now. One to three paragraphs. The version you'd explain over dinner to someone smart who isn't in tech. Define how, when, and why the user would experience this product.

## 2. The User and Their World

Real people, not personas. Name them if possible. Describe their current life with enough depth that a designer reading this feels like they KNOW these users.

For each user archetype:
- Who they are (real person, not category)
- What their [relevant domain] life looks like today
- What's broken or missing
- What they wish existed
- How they'd describe the problem in their own words (quote the owner)

## 3. The Key Moments

The 5-8 moments that define the product experience. Not screens, moments. Not features, feelings.

For each moment:
- **The moment:** What happens (one sentence)
- **What the user feels:** The emotional target
- **What makes it magic:** The specific detail that elevates this from functional to delightful
- **What makes it mundane:** The version every other app ships (the thing we're NOT doing)

## 4. The Reference Decomposition

For each external reference the owner has cited:

### [Reference Name] (e.g., Flighty, Slopes, Oura)

**What we're taking:**
- [Specific principle or pattern, described concretely]

**What we're NOT taking:**
- [Specific aspect that doesn't apply, with reason]

**How it translates to this product:**
- [Concrete application to a specific surface or interaction]

## 5. Design Intent

The emotional and visual direction. Not a design system (that comes later). The mood, the feeling, the anti-patterns.

- **The mood:** [sensory description, not abstract adjectives]
- **Visual references:** [named apps, websites, physical objects that capture the target feel]
- **What this is NOT:** [specific anti-patterns that would kill the vibe]
- **Key design principles:** [3-5 principles derived from the reference decomposition]

## 6. Scope and Anti-Scope

Framed as philosophy, not feature lists.

**What V1 is:**
[Philosophy statement that enables independent scope decisions]

**What V1 is not:**
[Explicit anti-scope with reasoning, not just "deferred to V2"]

## 7. Open Questions and Risks

What isn't locked. Named honestly. For each:
- The question
- Why it matters (what depends on the answer)
- Current best guess (if any)
```

## Process Ledger

Append Phase 5 with:
- **References decomposed:** list of external references processed with key takeaways
- **Design intent decisions:** mood, visual direction, anti-patterns locked
- **Scope philosophy statement:** the one-sentence scope test
- **Key moments identified:** count and brief summary of each
- **Gaps noted:** anything that couldn't be extracted and was routed to Open Questions

Update Provenance.

## Relationship to Other Phases

- **Feeds Phase 6 (Strategic Council):** The brief is the Council's primary input. Six independent advisors stress-test the strategy before execution begins.
- **Feeds Phase 7 (Component Map):** The Key Moments section tells you which surfaces need the most design attention. The Reference Decomposition tells you what interaction patterns to adopt.
- **Feeds Phase 8 (User Stories):** The User and Their World section provides the "As a [user]" framing. The Key Moments provide the acceptance bar ("magic" vs "mundane").
- **Feeds Phase 10 (Adversarial Review):** The brief is a companion document. If the PRD contradicts the vision brief's intent, that's a finding.
- **Feeds external processes:** Design consultation, marketing positioning, investor materials, team alignment.
